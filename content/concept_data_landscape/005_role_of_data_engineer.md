A **data engineer** is a key player in the field of data science and analytics and plays a crucial role in managing and preparing big data. Their primary job revolves around designing, constructing, installing, testing, and maintaining highly scalable data management systems. They work at the intersection of database technology, programming, and cloud-based technology. 

Data engineer takes roles and responsibilities regarding the following functions:

1. **Designing:** Data engineers design, construct, and manage large-scale, robust, and fault-tolerant data architectures. They also ensure that these architectures can handle business needs.
2. **Building:** They use different languages and tools to marry systems together or create APIs that interact with data systems and databases. Building and maintaining ETL (Extract-Transform-Load) pipelines are also part of their responsibility.
3. **Testing and Maintenance:** Data engineers ensure that systems meet business requirements, as well as industry practices. They maintain data security and privacy, which involves disaster recovery planning.
4. **Collaboration:** They frequently work alongside data scientists, data analysts, and management to ensure that they have the data necessary for their work. They understand and translate business needs into data models, supporting the rest of the data team with data-related technical issues.

## Demand for data engineers

The demand for data engineers has rised with the recent developments in technology as they enable to deal with data more efficiently. Today, industries across the board are becoming data-focused due to the increasing recognition of data as crucial to decision-making and forecasting. With the advent of technologies like Machine Learning, Artificial Intelligence, companies are dealing with larger amounts and varieties of data. To make these data useful and gain insights from them, skilled data engineers are required. Fields such as healthcare, finance, retail, and telecommunications, in particular, have observed a high demand for data engineers.

Another factor driving this demand is the increasing trend of moving to cloud-based solutions. As more companies migrate their data to the cloud, the need for data engineers who can build and maintain cloud-based data systems is also growing. As a part of these functions, data security, privacy, and compliance are pivotal considerations in data science, given the sensitive nature of the data often handled. Each of these aspects are discussed as follows:

**Data Security:** This pertains to protective measures taken to safeguard data from unauthorized access, alteration, or destruction. Measures often include encryption during data storage and transmission, secure user authentication protocols, network security measures (like firewalls), and regular security audits. A formal data security policy often provides guidance on these measures and how to handle various data types.

**Data Privacy:** Data privacy concerns are largely about ensuring personal data is collected, stored, shared and used in ways that comply with regulations and respects an individual's choices regarding their personal information. Data privacy laws often cover aspects such as data anonymization, consent for data collection, data access rights and data breach notification rules. These principles ensure the confidentiality and privacy of users' data.

**Compliance:** Compliance refers to how well the practices, policies, and operations of a company align with established rules, regulations, and standards governing data use, security, and privacy. Different industries, countries, and regions may have differing compliance requirements. For example, General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) is a regulation in EU law on data protection and privacy. Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) is a US law designed to protect sensitive patient health information. Firms must be compliant with these legal frameworks to avoid penalties, legal ramifications, and reputational damage.

Data scientists and engineers need to consider these areas throughout the cycle of a data project, from data collection, storage, analysis, to data sharing and destruction. Specifically, they need to handle personal, confidential or secure data correctly, anonymising, pseudonymising or aggregating it where needed. Practising ethical data use is a key aspect of data science.
